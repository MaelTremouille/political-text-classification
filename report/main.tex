\begin{abstract}
In this project, we try to predict the political affiliation of candidates from their \textit{professions de foi} (campaign manifestos) in French legislative elections between 1973 and 1993.
We use 4{,}519 documents from the Archelec corpus, extract party labels with regex, and classify them into four political families.
A first run with TF-IDF + logistic regression gets 84\% test accuracy and a fine-tuned CamemBERT reaches 71\%, but a close look at the top TF-IDF features shows that the model is relying a lot on stopwords, election dates, place names and politicians' surnames rather than on political vocabulary. We run a second cleaning pass that masks these categories and re-evaluate with cross-validation, bootstrap confidence intervals, and temporal splits. Test accuracy stays at 84\% on the i.i.d.\ split but drops to 72\% when we train on 1973--1981 and test on 1988--1993, which indicates that a non-negligible part of the signal is regional or cohort-specific rather than ideological.
We also try a data-efficient alternative to full fine-tuning: sentence-CamemBERT used as a frozen encoder with a linear head on top. It only reaches 67\% test accuracy, below TF-IDF, which suggests that on this corpus the lexical signal is hard to beat with contextual embeddings.
Finally, we visualise inter-party distances using sentence-transformer embeddings with both t-SNE and UMAP, and we quantify cluster separation with silhouette and Davies--Bouldin scores. The result confirms what the classifier already suggested: political families overlap heavily in the embedding space, except for Extr\^{e}me gauche which sits in a relatively tight cluster.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

In France, \textit{professions de foi} are official campaign statements distributed to all registered voters before legislative elections. Because they follow a relatively standard format across parties and years, they form an interesting corpus for studying political discourse.

We ask the following question: \textbf{can we predict a candidate's political affiliation from the text alone, without any explicit party mention?} If so, what features distinguish each political family? And how do parties relate to one another in a semantic embedding space?

We use the Archelec corpus~\citep{archelec}, which contains digitized and OCR'd \textit{professions de foi} from French legislative elections held in 1973, 1978, 1981, 1988, and 1993. The OCR'd text files were obtained directly from the course repository\footnote{\url{https://gitlab.teklia.com/ckermorvant/arkindex\_archelec}}, so we did not have to perform any OCR processing. This twenty-year period covers significant changes in the French political landscape, from left-wing coalitions in the 1970s to alternations of power in the 1980s and 1990s.

Our approach is as follows. First, we build a labeled dataset by extracting party mentions from the text using regular expressions, then removing these mentions to prevent data leakage (Section~\ref{sec:data}). Second, we compare a TF-IDF logistic regression baseline with a fine-tuned CamemBERT model~\citep{martin2020camembert} (Section~\ref{sec:models}). The initial TF-IDF baseline reaches 84\% test accuracy, but when we inspect its top features we find that the model is partly classifying on stopwords, election dates, place names, and politicians' surnames, not on political discourse. We therefore add a second preprocessing pipeline that masks these categories, re-evaluate under cross-validation and temporal splits, and introduce a third data-efficient model (frozen sentence-CamemBERT with a linear head) to test whether contextual representations can recover the signal we have just stripped from the TF-IDF inputs. Third, we analyse inter-party distances through sentence-transformer embeddings with t-SNE and UMAP, and add quantitative clustering metrics (Section~\ref{sec:results}). Implementation details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation}.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Text-based political analysis.}
Using text to analyse political positions has been explored in several directions. \citet{grimmer2013text} give a general overview of automatic content analysis methods for political texts, and insist on the need to validate any text-based measure against ground truth. \citet{laver2003extracting} proposed Wordscores, a method that scores words from reference texts with known political positions and then uses those scores to place new texts on a left--right scale; they tested it on British and Irish party manifestos. \citet{slapin2008scaling} followed up with Wordfish, which scales parties on a single ideological dimension from word frequencies alone, without reference texts. On the empirical-political-science side, the Manifesto Project~\citep{volkens2013manifesto} built a large hand-coded database of party platforms across many countries, which is still widely used. Closer to our setup, \citet{peterson2018classification} use the classification accuracy of a party-vs-party classifier on Westminster speeches as a direct measure of how polarised the two sides are, which we find useful as a reading of our own Gauche--Droite confusion rate. More recently, \citet{rheault2020word} trained word embeddings with party metadata on parliamentary debates from Britain, Canada and the US and showed that it captures ideological differences.

What we do is somewhat related to Laver et al.\ and Slapin--Proksch, but instead of placing parties on a continuous scale we classify documents into four discrete families. We also work with French texts rather than English ones and we compare a TF-IDF baseline with a fine-tuned transformer and a frozen-encoder variant, which none of the above papers do.

\paragraph{NLP for French political texts.}
Most of this literature is focused on English. For French, there is less. The Archelec project~\citep{archelec} made a large corpus of \textit{professions de foi} available, but their own work is about digitisation and OCR, not classification. The MARPOR/CMP data~\citep{volkens2013manifesto} does include some French party programmes, but not at the candidate level.

\paragraph{CamemBERT and sentence-transformers.}
CamemBERT~\citep{martin2020camembert} is a RoBERTa-based model pretrained on French web data (OSCAR), which works well on standard French NLP benchmarks like NER, POS tagging, and text classification, which is why we picked it for our task. For the semantic mapping, we use a sentence-transformer~\citep{reimers2019sentence}: a BERT-style encoder that has been further trained so that cosine similarity between its pooled outputs reflects sentence-level semantic similarity. The variant we use (\texttt{dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large}) is a French instance of that family, built on CamemBERT-large.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Corpus description}

The Archelec corpus consists of scanned and OCR'd \textit{professions de foi} from French legislative elections. Each raw file corresponds to a single scanned page, following a naming convention that encodes metadata: election identifier, year, department code, constituency number, election round, and document type. It is then easy to build a database that reconstructs the whole documents using the available information.

We keep only \textit{professions de foi} (tagged \texttt{PF}), we delete ballots (\texttt{BV}) and OCR processing artifacts (\texttt{pdfmasterocr}). Multi-page documents belonging to the same candidate are concatenated in page order to reconstruct complete texts. After aggregation, the corpus contains 4{,}658 documents that cover five election years.

\subsection{Label extraction}
\label{sec:labels}

The corpus does not include structured party labels, but it seemed possible to directly extract them thanks to the normalized form of \textit{profession de foi}. We extract them from the text itself using a two-stage regex approach:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Full name patterns}: we search the entire text for explicit party names (e.g., \textit{Parti Communiste Fran\c{c}ais}, \textit{Rassemblement pour la R\'{e}publique}). Patterns handle OCR variants (accented and unaccented characters, variable spacing). They are ordered from most specific to least specific to avoid ambiguous matches (e.g., we search for \textit{Parti Socialiste Unifi\'{e}} before \textit{Parti Socialiste}, otherwise PSU texts would be mislabeled as PS).
    \item \textbf{Abbreviation patterns}: for remaining unlabeled documents, we search the first 1{,}000 characters for common acronyms (e.g., PS, RPR, UDF), restricting to the header area to limit false positives from opponents mentioned in the text body. It allowed us to narrow the proportion of unlabeled texts to about 2\%.
\end{enumerate}

This procedure labels 97.8\% of documents (4{,}557 out of 4{,}658). We identify 17 distinct party labels, which we group into five political families: \textit{Extr\^{e}me gauche} (LO, LCR), \textit{Gauche} (PCF\footnote{The PCF could arguably be classified as \textit{Extr\^{e}me gauche}. We place it in \textit{Gauche} because, during the 1973--1993 period, the PCF was part of the Union de la Gauche coalition with the PS and participated in government (1981--1984).}, PS, PSU, MRG), \textit{\'{E}cologistes} (Verts), \textit{Droite} (RPR, UDF, UDR, RI, and coalition labels), and \textit{Extr\^{e}me droite} (FN, PFN).

We do not take into account the \'{E}cologistes class (38 documents), which is too small for meaningful classification (we could not classify them well without decreasing the overall results too much). The final dataset contains \textbf{4{,}519 documents} over four political families.

\paragraph{Data leakage prevention.}
Since we aim to classify political affiliation from discourse content rather than explicit party names, we remove all party mentions from the text using the same regex patterns. This produces a first cleaned column, which we call \texttt{text\_clean}, used in the baseline.

\paragraph{A second cleaning pass.}
\label{sec:textcleanv2}
Running the baseline exposed a gap between what we wanted to measure (political discourse) and what the model actually uses. The top TF-IDF coefficients include a lot of stopwords, election dates (\textit{``12 juin''}, \textit{``mars 1988''}, \textit{``1981''}), French toponyms (\textit{``guadeloupe''}, \textit{``corse''}, \textit{``metz''}, \textit{``nice''}), politicians' surnames that the original cleaning did not remove (\textit{``le pen''}, \textit{``cercle national''}), ideological label words (\textit{``communiste''}) and a few German tokens (\textit{``die''}, \textit{``und''}) that probably come from bilingual manifestos in Alsace or Moselle. Most of these have little to do with the political content we wanted to classify. We therefore build a second cleaned column, \texttt{text\_clean\_v2}, by successively masking (i) a list of common French stopwords (extended with the German tokens just mentioned); (ii) dates via regex on \{day + month\}, \{month + year\}, and standalone 19XX years; (iii) a hardcoded list of around 220 French toponyms, covering the metropolitan and overseas departments, the historical regions, and the largest cities; (iv) an extended list of politicians' surnames (Mitterrand, Chirac, Le~Pen, etc.) and coalition banners (\textit{Cercle National}, \textit{Majorité Présidentielle}, etc.). We evaluate TF-IDF on \texttt{text\_clean\_v2}, but the transformer models still use \texttt{text\_clean} because attention models rely on the full sentence structure (removing stopwords would hurt them).

\section{Models}
\label{sec:models}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We frame political affiliation prediction as a classification task with four classes. We train three models: a TF-IDF logistic regression baseline, an end-to-end fine-tuned CamemBERT, and a data-efficient variant that uses sentence-CamemBERT as a frozen encoder with a linear head.

\subsection{Baseline: TF-IDF + logistic regression}

We represent each document as a TF-IDF vector with up to 20{,}000 features, including unigrams and bigrams. We set a minimum document frequency of~3 to filter out OCR artifacts and typos.

We train a logistic regression with L2 regularization and balanced class weights, so that minority classes like Extr\^{e}me droite (only 43 training examples) are not ignored.

This baseline is fast to train and sees the \emph{entire} document regardless of length, unlike transformer models which truncate inputs.

\subsection{CamemBERT fine-tuning (end-to-end)}

We fine-tune \texttt{camembert-base}~\citep{martin2020camembert} (110M parameters) for sequence classification. CamemBERT is a RoBERTa-based model pretrained on French web data with masked language modeling, so unlike TF-IDF it produces contextual representations. We use the \texttt{[CLS]} token output as a document representation and add a linear layer on top for classification. Inputs are truncated to 512 tokens, which is a hard limit of the architecture.

To handle class imbalance, we use a weighted cross-entropy loss where each
class receives a weight inversely proportional to its frequency, following
the same logic as the balanced logistic regression. Note that at this stage
we had already removed the \'Ecologistes class, because giving it a very
high weight (to compensate for only 38 documents) was destabilizing the
training. We explored several training configurations (Table~\ref{tab:configs}),
varying the number of epochs and the use of a linear learning rate scheduler:
the learning rate starts at zero, increases linearly during the first 10\%
of training steps (warmup), then decreases back to zero. This helps avoid
large gradient updates early in training when the model has not yet adapted
to the task. Additional configurations that did not improve results are
detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:training}.

All runs use AdamW with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$, weight decay of 0.01, and a batch size of 8. Training was done on an Apple M-series GPU (MPS backend), which was enough given the small dataset size.

\subsection{Frozen sentence-CamemBERT with a linear head}
\label{sec:frozen}

Fine-tuning a 110M-parameter transformer on 3{,}163 training examples overfits (we show this in Section~\ref{sec:results}). To stay in the same transformer family while matching the small-data regime, we add a third model: a \emph{frozen} sentence-CamemBERT encoder with a small linear head.

We reuse the \texttt{dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large} model that we already use for the semantic mapping. It outputs a 1{,}024-dimensional vector per document (mean-pooled over token hidden states of a CamemBERT-large encoder that was further trained on a French sentence-similarity dataset\footnote{See the model card at \url{https://huggingface.co/dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large}.}). We do not update any of its parameters on our corpus. On top of these frozen vectors we fit a logistic regression with balanced class weights, the same classifier as the TF-IDF baseline. This drops the number of trainable parameters from $\sim$110M to about 4{,}100 (1024~$\times$~4 classes plus biases), which turns the comparison against TF-IDF + LR into a comparison of \emph{representations}: both feed a linear head of similar capacity, one with 20{,}000 sparse lexical features, the other with 1{,}024 dense contextual features.

\subsection{Semantic mapping}

Beyond classification, we also want to see how close or far parties are from each other in terms of discourse. The idea is to represent each document as an embedding and then compare them.

We first tried using the \texttt{[CLS]} vector from the pretrained CamemBERT base model (without fine-tuning), but this did not work: all pairwise cosine similarities exceeded 0.98, so every party looked identical (see Appendix~\ref{app:cls_failure}) meaning the model treated all discourses the same. The reason is that CamemBERT base was trained to predict masked words, not to produce embeddings that reflect meaning, and because all \textit{professions de foi} share the same formal register, the \texttt{[CLS]} vectors just converge and give similar values.

We then switched to a sentence-transformer\footnote{\texttt{dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large}, available at \url{https://huggingface.co/dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large}.}, which is built on CamemBERT-large and further trained for sentence-level semantic similarity. We picked the large variant because we do not fine-tune it (size is not a problem at inference time) and it was the most widely used French sentence-transformer at the time of the project. It uses mean pooling over all token embeddings instead of just the \texttt{[CLS]} token, and the resulting vectors gave us much more usable pairwise distances.

We then compute mean embeddings per party and per political family, and use cosine similarity to compare them. For visualization, we project everything to 2D with t-SNE. As usual with t-SNE, local neighborhoods are preserved but global distances can be distorted, so the plots should be read with some caution.

What surprised us first is that the v2 cleaning does not really change the test score. Removing stopwords, election dates, around 220 toponyms and our extended list of politicians' surnames only moves accuracy from 0.841 to 0.842 (and CV from 0.835 to 0.850). At first we thought a drop was coming, since we were pulling out many of the features that the model had been leaning on; the fact that accuracy stays flat is reassuring, but it is also a bit of a warning. If the score does not move, then we did not actually remove the discriminative signal, we just shifted where it hides. The feature inspection below confirms this: after v2, the top features are still partly regional, with local candidate surnames and minor city names that our hardcoded toponym list did not cover. A more rigorous cleaning would need a proper French NER and a full toponym gazetteer, and that was out of scope for this sprint.

Both transformer variants do worse than TF-IDF. The frozen sentence-CamemBERT head only reaches 66.8\% on the test set, and even fine-tuning the whole CamemBERT-base stops at 71\% despite its 110M parameters. This is consistent with the feature analysis: the signal is mostly lexical, and 20{,}000 sparse TF-IDF features capture that more directly than a 1{,}024-dimensional semantic vector which averages all tokens together. \citet{laver2003extracting} already made this point on British and Irish manifestos, that for short political texts with clear lexical markers, word frequencies often suffice. The frozen head is the comparison we think is fairest to TF-IDF in terms of parameter count (both have a linear head on top of a fixed representation), and the fact that it still loses suggests the gap is more about the representation than about any overfitting issue during fine-tuning.

Finally, the 5-fold CV intervals are narrow ($\pm$0.01 on accuracy for both TF-IDF variants and for the frozen head), so the gap between models is not noise from a lucky split.

The biggest problem is the confusion between Gauche and Droite: 76--80 Gauche documents are predicted as Droite, and 12--15 Droite ones end up classified as Gauche (the range matches across v1 and v2). This is not so surprising when you think about it: once you take out party names, left and right candidates often say the same kind of things about employment, housing or education, with quite similar words. On the other hand, Extr\^{e}me gauche is almost never confused with other families (4 errors out of 175).

If we look at precision and recall, the same thing shows up differently. Droite has a recall of 0.77--0.80 but a precision of only 0.45--0.48, which means the model finds most Droite documents but it also places a lot of Gauche texts into the Droite category. The line between mainstream left and right is genuinely blurry in these texts, a point we return to in the discussion. For Extr\^{e}me gauche it is the opposite: precision 0.94--0.98 and recall 0.98, so the model almost never gets it wrong.

\paragraph{What words drive the classification: before and after cleaning.}
Looking at the logistic regression coefficients tells us what each model actually picks up on. We use a one-vs-rest scheme, so there is one logistic regression per class, each with 20{,}000 coefficients (one per TF-IDF feature); a positive coefficient pushes the prediction towards that class. We rank features by the signed score $\text{coef} \times \text{idf}$, which reflects how much a feature contributes in a typical document. The full top~20 per class for each cleaning variant is in Appendix~\ref{app:features}; we summarise the change below.

\textit{Baseline (\texttt{text\_clean}).} The baseline appendix table speaks for itself: the top features are dominated by stopwords and function words that rank high mainly because Extr\^{e}me gauche documents are much longer (``les'', ``de'', ``des'', ``il''), election dates (``12 juin'', ``juin 1988'', ``mars 1993''), toponyms (``guadeloupe'', ``la r\'{e}union'', ``corse'', ``metz'', ``nice''), politicians' surnames we did not strip (``le pen'', ``pen''), coalition labels (``cercle national''), and even German stopwords (``die'', ``und'') that most likely come from bilingual manifestos in Alsace or Moselle. Only a handful of genuinely political terms appear in the top~20 across classes (``travailleurs'' for the far-left, ``immigration'' for the far-right). In other words, the baseline's 84\% accuracy is built on a mix of electoral geography, a bit of political vocabulary and some document-length effects, not really on the political discourse we wanted to classify.

\textit{Cleaned (\texttt{text\_clean\_v2}).} After the second cleaning pass the Extr\^{e}me gauche list becomes much more political: \textit{travailleurs}, \textit{patrons}, \textit{bourgeoisie}, \textit{travailleuses}, \textit{révolutionnaires}, \textit{petits}, \textit{payer}, \textit{maintenir emplois} are all typical of LO and LCR class-struggle rhetoric. This is a real improvement: we can now look at the model's features and recognise political content. For the Gauche class we see similar shifts towards more political features (\textit{programme commun}, \textit{union gauche}, \textit{majorité france}).

For Droite and Extr\^{e}me droite, the cleaning exposes a second layer of leakage we did not anticipate: \emph{local candidates}. The top Droite features now include surnames that were not in our hardcoded politician list (\textit{Klifa}, \textit{Bousquet}, \textit{Zimmermann}, \textit{Rosières}, \textit{Simon}) and minor cities outside the top~100 we hardcoded (\textit{Cotentin}, \textit{Vendôme}, \textit{Rive}), plus overseas-territory vocabulary (\textit{réunionnais}, \textit{martiniquais}) that we did not treat as toponyms. The Extr\^{e}me droite list looks similar, with even more surnames (\textit{Robien}, \textit{Masson}, \textit{Guyard}, \textit{Debré}) and region-specific terms (\textit{Evry}, \textit{pays basque}). For these two classes we mostly shifted the leakage from dates/places to people and minor cities. One plausible reading, which we would need more work to confirm, is that the right-wing portion of the corpus is more \emph{candidate-centric} than the left-wing one: mainstream-right \textit{professions de foi} seem to build identity around the local incumbent more than around a national ideological vocabulary. We did not have time to integrate a French named-entity recogniser or a full INSEE commune gazetteer, and we flag this as a limitation (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Temporal generalisation}
\label{sec:temporal}

The held-out test split above is i.i.d.: train and test documents are drawn from the same election years. But the corpus spans twenty years of French politics, which is a long time over which both party landscapes and candidates' discourse change. To test how much of the model's performance relies on era-specific signal rather than on stable ideology, we re-run TF-IDF and the frozen head after re-splitting the corpus by year. Table~\ref{tab:temporal} reports the results.

      \multicolumn{2}{c}{TF-IDF v2 + LR} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Frozen SCaM + LR} \\

The picture changes a lot once the split stops being i.i.d. TF-IDF drops from 0.842 on the held-out test to 0.72 when we train on 1973--1981 and test on 1988--1993, and further to 0.59 when we train up to 1988 and test on 1993 only. 1993 turns out to be the hardest year: the ballot composition is quite different from the earlier ones (the RPR and UDF ran joint candidates under the Union pour la France banner, and the FN reached its legislative peak at about 12\% of the vote), so the classifier ends up applying features learned on older coalition structures to a reshaped ballot. The per-year numbers from Appendix~\ref{app:errors} confirm the pattern: TF-IDF test accuracy sits above 0.90 on 1973, 1978 and 1981, drops to 0.71 on 1988 and 0.79 on 1993 under the i.i.d.\ split, and the drop on 1993 becomes much sharper under the temporal split.

The frozen head is hit roughly twice as hard. Its accuracy loses about 25 points between the i.i.d.\ test (0.668) and the temporal test (0.422), compared to roughly 12 points for TF-IDF. One tentative reading: TF-IDF captures fine-grained lexical markers, some of which are probably stable across elections (the class-struggle vocabulary of LO/LCR is our best candidate for a stable marker), while the sentence embedding compresses the document into a summary of its \emph{content} (employment, immigration, foreign policy), and content topics drift faster across eras than isolated ideological words do. We would need to check coefficient stability per year to really confirm this, so we leave it as a hypothesis.

Either way, the i.i.d.\ 0.842 probably overstates what we would get on a new election. Something like 0.72 is closer to what one should expect if, say, the method were used to pre-label Archelec documents from a year not yet transcribed.

\paragraph{Error analysis by length, year and OCR quality.}
We cross the held-out test predictions with three document-level covariates to see where the models succeed and fail (Figure~\ref{fig:err_buckets}). Three patterns stand out. First, accuracy increases with document length for TF-IDF: 0.56 on short documents ($<$500 tokens), 0.68 on 500--1{,}500, 0.95 on 1{,}500--3{,}000, and 0.97 above 3{,}000. Longer manifestos give TF-IDF more lexical evidence, and since Extr\^{e}me gauche documents are on average much longer than Droite ones (Table~\ref{tab:lengths}), the length effect probably helps explain the high F1 on that class. The frozen head follows the same trend but flatter, which makes sense for a fixed-dimensional embedding that averages over all tokens. Second, across years the TF-IDF test accuracy peaks in 1973--1981 (above 0.90) and dips at 1988 (0.71), which matches the political realignment of the decade (the \textit{alternance} of 1981, the cohabitation of 1986--1988, and the FN's rise in vote share from 1988 onwards). Third, the OCR-quality proxy we tried (ratio of non-alphabetic characters) is not discriminative: almost all documents fall below 10\%, so we cannot separate clean from noisy OCR this way. A better proxy would use token-level anomalies or the OCR confidence from the Teklia pipeline, which we do not have with the raw text files we received.

\paragraph{Why end-to-end fine-tuning does not help.}
CamemBERT overfits quickly: the training loss keeps going down (from 1.18 to 0.43 in 5 epochs) but the validation accuracy plateaus after epoch 2 at around 72\%. With only 3{,}163 training documents for 110M parameters, this is not too surprising. The frozen head we added (Section~\ref{sec:frozen}) is a natural control: same pretrained encoder, much fewer trainable parameters, and the result is actually \emph{worse} on the i.i.d.\ test (0.668 vs 0.71). So the fine-tuning gain of about 4 accuracy points over the frozen head is real but small, and comes with a clear overfitting risk. On this corpus, the main signal seems to be lexical (which specific words appear), and TF-IDF + LR captures that more directly than either transformer variant. On top of that, the 512-token truncation cuts most of the long Extr\^{e}me gauche manifestos (which average $\sim$6{,}700 words), and the Extr\^{e}me droite class only has 43 training examples, both of which make things harder for the transformer.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Our main accuracy number does not change much between the baseline and the cleaned setup (84\% $\to$ 84\%), but what that accuracy actually measures does. Before the cleaning, TF-IDF reached 84\% by relying on a mix of election dates, toponyms, politicians' surnames and a few real ideological markers. After the cleaning, it still reaches 84\%, but the top features for Extr\^{e}me gauche are now \textit{travailleurs}, \textit{patrons}, \textit{bourgeoisie}, \textit{révolutionnaires}, \textit{travailleuses}: typical LO/LCR vocabulary. For that class at least, the model is now picking up on political content in the way we originally wanted. \citet{laver2003extracting} observed something similar on British and Irish manifestos: short political texts are already separable at the word-frequency level, without any contextual model. We see the same pattern on French \textit{professions de foi}, and we also found that how you decide what words count as ``informative'' changes the interpretation of the score quite a lot.

For the Droite and Extr\^{e}me droite classes, the outcome is more humbling. Even after removing around 220 toponyms and 20+ politician names, the top features are still mostly local candidates' surnames (Klifa, Bousquet, Zimmermann, Debré, Robien, Masson, Guyard, \ldots) and minor place names (Cotentin, Vendôme, Evry, pays basque). The leakage basically shifted from the category we had anticipated (Mitterrand/Chirac, Paris/Marseille) to a secondary one we had not. A more reproducible cleaning would probably need a French NER model and a full INSEE commune gazetteer; a handcrafted wordlist is not enough. More interesting for us was the realisation that this regional/candidate-level signal is also a real feature of French legislative elections: each candidate campaigns in one constituency, is often a local notable, and their \textit{profession de foi} mentions themselves, their township, and local issues. It looks like the right-wing portion of the corpus is more candidate-centric than the left-wing one (which has a stronger national ideological vocabulary), but we would need an annotated sample to confirm this rather than infer it from a few dozen features. Still, the ``leakage'' is partly a property of the object itself, and a classifier that ignored all of it would lose real signal.

The Gauche--Droite confusion (76--80 false Gauche$\to$Droite predictions, 12--15 the other way) is the classifier's biggest error source and, we think, the most interesting finding from a political-science angle. It matches a well-documented phenomenon: after the 1981 \textit{alternance} and the first cohabitation (1986--1988), the Parti Socialiste and the mainstream right started to use overlapping vocabulary on common themes (employment, housing, modernisation of public services). The per-party trajectories in Figure~\ref{fig:trajectories} show this: the RPR barycentre moves a lot across elections, while the PCF's stays close to itself. From an NLP standpoint, this means the four classes are not equidistant: Extr\^{e}me gauche is a tight cluster with a fixed vocabulary (high precision and recall), while Gauche and Droite overlap a lot in the embedding space (the negative silhouette in Table~\ref{tab:clustering} makes this quantitative), which no linear model can cleanly separate without relying on non-political signals.

The temporal-split drop (Section~\ref{sec:temporal}) tells a similar story in numbers. When train and test share elections, both models benefit from era-specific signals; when they do not, TF-IDF loses 12 accuracy points and the frozen head loses 25. The fact that the frozen head is hit harder is probably informative: sentence embeddings summarise a document into a topical representation, and the topics themselves shift across a 20-year period; TF-IDF, being lexical, keeps hold of a few more stable markers (the far-left vocabulary is our best candidate). A natural line of work for small historical corpora, that we did not have time to try, would be to combine sparse lexical and dense semantic features and to evaluate explicitly with a temporal split.

On the semantic mapping itself: we first tried the CamemBERT base \texttt{[CLS]} token, which collapsed every profession de foi into a near-identical vector (all pairwise cosines above 0.98, see Appendix~\ref{app:cls_failure}), because CamemBERT-base is trained for masked language modelling and does not produce document-level embeddings where cosine similarity is informative. Switching to \texttt{dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large} --- a CamemBERT-large further trained on a French sentence-similarity dataset --- was what made the geometry usable. The cosine heatmap and the UMAP both agree with the classifier's confusion matrix: most parties sit above 0.90 cosine to each other, RPCR (New Caledonia) is the only clear outlier, and silhouette is slightly negative even on the raw 1{,}024-dimensional embeddings. The space seems to encode discourse \emph{style} more than ideological programme.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
A few things about our labels and data are worth flagging. Our regex labelling only works when the party name is actually written in the text. Left-wing parties like PCF or LO almost always state their name, while right-wing candidates often run under coalition labels or do not mention a party at all, which is the main reason for the class imbalance. OCR quality is also not uniform across years (older manifestos are noisier), and the non-alphabetic-ratio proxy we tried is not discriminative enough to confirm a quantitative effect. A better OCR-quality score would read the confidence values from the Teklia pipeline, which are not included in the text files we received.

Even after the v2 cleaning, the TF-IDF classifier still leans on local candidate surnames and minor city names. We hardcoded the 95+ French departments, the main historical regions and roughly a hundred cities; scaling to the 36,000 or so INSEE communes would need a downloadable gazetteer, and a French NER model would catch the candidate names we missed. We did not have time to plug either of those in. Separately, the text-length gap between families (Table~\ref{tab:lengths}) means TF-IDF partly captures length: a 6,000-word document accumulates more high-idf evidence than a 1,000-word one, we did not length-normalise, and this contributes to Extr\^{e}me gauche being easier to classify than Droite.

On the model side, CamemBERT-base only reads the first 512 tokens, which penalises long Extr\^{e}me gauche manifestos (mean ${\sim}6{,}700$ words). A cheap next step would be to split documents into 512-token windows and pool logits across them; we did not implement this. We also did not try continued masked-language-model pretraining on the corpus. The 1973--1993 French political register is quite different from the web data CamemBERT was pretrained on, so domain-adaptive pretraining is probably the other obvious improvement for both the fine-tuned and the frozen variants, especially for temporal generalisation.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We started from a fairly standard result --- TF-IDF at 84\%, CamemBERT fine-tuned at 71\% on \textit{professions de foi} --- and tried to understand what that 84\% actually measures. Looking at the top features showed a mix of election dates, toponyms and politicians' surnames hiding under the score; a second cleaning pass pushed most of the Extr\^{e}me gauche top features back to a proper political vocabulary, while also exposing a second layer of signal we did not expect: French legislative elections are candidate-centric, so a \textit{profession de foi} mentions the candidate and their township, and these local references are correlated with party family. A data-efficient frozen sentence-CamemBERT head with about 4{,}100 trainable parameters does worse than TF-IDF (67\% vs 84\%) and serves as a control for the fine-tuned run, suggesting that the 84\% ceiling is set by the representation itself rather than by the classifier on top. A temporal split cuts TF-IDF accuracy from 84\% to 72\% and the frozen head from 67\% to 42\%, which is probably closer to what we should expect if we wanted to apply the classifier to a new election. Finally, silhouette and Davies--Bouldin on the sentence embeddings confirm numerically what t-SNE already suggested: political families overlap heavily in the embedding space, with Extr\^{e}me gauche being the only really tight cluster.

The most interesting finding, from a political-science point of view, is the Gauche--Droite overlap: the two classes share a lot of vocabulary, which matches the observation that after the 1981 \textit{alternance} the mainstream left and right converge stylistically. Conversely, the stability of the PCF/LO vocabulary across the 20 years covered by the corpus is what keeps those classes cleanly separable.

We list a few natural next steps that were out of scope here: a proper French NER plus an INSEE commune gazetteer to remove the second-order leakage; continued masked-language-model pretraining of CamemBERT on the corpus to adapt the encoder to 1970--1990s French political language; a chunk-pooled fine-tune to bypass the 512-token limit; and matching candidates against the Archelec metadata (if available) to get ground-truth affiliations, which would reduce the dependence on our regex labels, especially on the minority right-wing classes. A follow-up on more recent \textit{professions de foi} (2017, 2022) would also extend the trajectory analysis to present-day French politics.

\section{Implementation details}
\label{app:implementation}

All code is available on GitHub\footnote{\url{https://github.com/MaelTremouille/political-text-classification}}. The pipeline consists of the following scripts:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{src/data\_preparation.py}: parses raw text files, filters \textit{professions de foi}, aggregates multi-page documents, produces \texttt{data/corpus\_by\_document.parquet}.
    \item \texttt{src/label\_extraction.py}: extracts party labels via regex, removes party-name mentions from text (producing \texttt{text\_clean}), assigns political families, and creates stratified train/val/test splits (seed 42).
    \item \texttt{src/preprocessing.py}: builds the aggressively-cleaned \texttt{text\_clean\_v2} column by successively masking stopwords, dates, $\sim$220 hardcoded toponyms (departments, regions, major cities), and an extended list of politicians' surnames.
    \item \texttt{src/classification.py}: trains the TF-IDF baseline and fine-tunes CamemBERT on \texttt{text\_clean}, evaluates both on the test set, appends to \texttt{data/results.txt}. (Unchanged from the initial run; the fine-tuned CamemBERT numbers quoted in the paper come from this script's latest run.)
    \item \texttt{src/evaluation.py}: re-runs TF-IDF + LogReg on an arbitrary text column (\texttt{text\_clean} or \texttt{text\_clean\_v2}), reports held-out test metrics with bootstrap CI, 5-fold stratified CV, three temporal splits, and the top-20 features per class.
    \item \texttt{src/frozen\_classifier.py}: fits a logistic regression on pre-computed sentence-CamemBERT embeddings (no transformer parameter is updated), runs the same three protocols.
    \item \texttt{src/political\_mapping.py}: extracts sentence-transformer embeddings, t-SNE projection, cosine heatmap (unchanged from initial run).
    \item \texttt{src/semantic\_mapping\_v2.py}: UMAP projection, silhouette + Davies--Bouldin metrics, per-party temporal trajectories.
    \item \texttt{src/error\_analysis.py}: breaks down test accuracy by document length, election year, and OCR-quality proxy for both TF-IDF~v2 and the frozen head.
\end{enumerate}

CamemBERT fine-tuning was performed on an Apple M-series chip (MPS backend). Each training epoch takes approximately 30 minutes. All the data-efficient variants (preprocessing, TF-IDF, frozen classifier, UMAP, error analysis, 5-fold CV, bootstrap CI, temporal splits) run on CPU in under 15~minutes combined, on the same machine.

\section{CamemBERT training configurations}
\label{app:training}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:models}, we tested multiple CamemBERT configurations to improve performance:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Without class weighting} (5 classes, 3 epochs): the model predicts the majority class (Gauche) for most inputs, achieving 75\% accuracy but 0\% recall on minority classes.
    \item \textbf{With class weighting} (5 classes, 3 epochs): weighted cross-entropy loss forces attention to minority classes, but \'{E}cologistes (weight $= 23.6\times$) destabilizes training. Test accuracy: 60\%.
    \item \textbf{4 classes, weighted, no scheduler} (3 epochs): removing \'{E}cologistes stabilizes training. Test accuracy: 68\%.
    \item \textbf{4 classes, weighted, with scheduler} (5 epochs): linear warmup and decay of learning rate. Test accuracy: 71\%. Overfitting observed from epoch 3 onward (training loss decreases while validation accuracy stagnates).
\end{itemize}

In all cases, the TF-IDF baseline (84\%) outperforms CamemBERT.

\section{CamemBERT base embeddings}
\label{app:cls_failure}

As mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:mapping}, our initial approach for semantic mapping used the \texttt{[CLS]} token embedding from the pretrained (not fine-tuned) CamemBERT base model. We computed cosine similarity between mean embeddings per party and found that all pairwise similarities exceeded 0.98 (Figure~\ref{fig:cls_heatmap}). The resulting heatmap was uninformative: all parties appeared equally similar.

This is because CamemBERT base was trained for masked language modeling, not for producing sentence-level semantic representations. Its \texttt{[CLS]} token is not optimized for semantic similarity, and since all \textit{professions de foi} share a similar formal French register, their representations converge.

We addressed this by switching to a sentence-transformer model (\texttt{dangvantuan/sentence-camembert-large}) trained specifically for semantic similarity, which uses mean pooling over all tokens rather than the \texttt{[CLS]} token alone. The resulting heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:cosine}) shows meaningful variation (0.70--1.00), validating this choice.

\section{Top 20 TF-IDF features per class: before and after cleaning}
\label{app:features}

We report the 20 features with the highest $\text{coef} \times \text{idf}$ score for each political family, under both cleaning pipelines. Because our TF-IDF uses both unigrams and bigrams, some features are single words (``travailleurs'') and others are bigrams (``france unie''; marked with * in Table~\ref{tab:top_features}).

\paragraph{Baseline (\texttt{text\_clean}): where the 84\% came from.} Table~\ref{tab:top_features} is dominated by three categories that have nothing to do with political discourse: stopwords and function words (``les'', ``de'', ``des'', ``il'', ``la'', which rank high for Extr\^{e}me gauche mostly because these manifestos are much longer than others), election dates (``12 juin'', ``juin 1988'', ``mars 1993''), French place names (``guadeloupe'', ``la r\'{e}union'', ``corse'', ``metz'', ``nice'', ``haute sa\^{o}ne''). Politicians' surnames (``le pen'', ``pen'') and the word ``cercle national'' leak through because we only masked full party names. German tokens (``die'', ``und'') come from bilingual Alsatian manifestos. Only a handful of genuinely political features appear: ``travailleurs'' for the far-left, ``immigration'' and ``fran\c{c}ais abord'' (\textit{French first}) for the far-right.

\paragraph{After v2 cleaning: a political top-20 for the far-left, local leakage for the right.} Table~\ref{tab:top_features_v2} shows the same ranking after the v2 pipeline removes stopwords, dates, the 101 departments + major cities, and an extended list of politicians' surnames. The Extr\^{e}me gauche column is now political: \textit{travailleurs}, \textit{patrons}, \textit{maintenir emplois}, \textit{bourgeoisie}, \textit{travailleuses}, \textit{payer}, \textit{r\'{e}volutionnaires}, \textit{petits} are all textbook LO/LCR markers. The Gauche column mixes stable programme-level terms (\textit{union gauche}, \textit{programme commun}, \textit{appellent}, \textit{majorit\'{e} france}) with Hautes-Alpes / Morlaix / Briey / Montb\'{e}liard style micro-toponyms we did not catch. The Droite and Extr\^{e}me droite columns remain heavily contaminated by local-candidate surnames (Klifa, Bousquet, Zimmermann, Robien, Masson, Guyard, Debr\'{e}) and minor place names (Cotentin, Vend\^{o}me, Rive, Evry, pays basque). We discuss this residual signal in Section~\ref{sec:results}: it is not only leakage but also a real feature of French legislative elections, where mainstream-right candidates build identity around the local incumbent more than around a national ideological vocabulary.
